PROSTHESIS DEVICES AND EQUIPMENT
BOW ARM



Your bow arm provides most of the shot's direction and bears the power of the draw as well as the bow's weight. As a result, shooting with proper bone alignment and a healthy muscle load is known to improve your score in a clear and positive way.


PROSTHESIS



A bow arm prosthesis strapped to the bow.

Prosthetic limbs If the archer's bow arm is amputated, a prosthesis with a device to carry the bow may be mounted. They may already have one that they can adapt for this reason, as it is used for daily tasks.


ELBOW SPLINT



An elbow splint supporting the bow arm

An elbow splint can be used if the bow arm is too frail to support the weight of the bow with the arm held out towards the target.


STRAPPING



Making use of strapping to hold the bow

The bow may be strapped to the hand if the bow arm is very weak or the beginner has no grip in their fingers. This strapping must be extremely tight to hold the bow in place, but it should be tested regularly to ensure that it is not cutting off the blood supply to the fingers.


DRAW ARM



It indicates that the archer has drawn the bow, entered their anchor point and shifted completely into alignment to feel the bow’s force through their bone strucrure and back.


PROSTHESIS



Trigger activated by the opening of the angle of the elbow join

A prosthesis can be installed if the drawing hand, wrist, or a portion of the forearm is missing. This can be equipped with a hook that connects to the string. To loosen the hook from the loop, the novice twists his forearm.


Trigger activated by the change of the orientation of the top body


An archer pulling the string with his mouth.

In the event of a more serious amputation, there are two common options.

A release aid that is attached to a body harness and can be triggered by rotating the top body or pressing the jaw on the button.


The beginner may also use their teeth to draw the bow. This is something that a lot of archers do. On either side of the nocking point, a piece of leather is tied to the string. The archer draws the string with their teeth and then releases the arrow by opening their mouth.


This "mouthtab" must be tested regularly because the archer will inevitably bite through it.



An archer using a release aid with a cheek trigger

If the drawing arm is weak and the fingers have no grip, the technique of strapping a hook to the hand has been used for many years and is still useful for beginners. With a twisting motion, the novice hooks this on the string to draw.


This type of release aid is common among archers because it is simple to make and use. If the archer's fingers aren't dexterity enough to release normally, a release aid with a trigger can be modified to activate when the trigger comes into contact with something, such as a certain part of the jaw.


Balance Problems



Archers using a stool or chair for support

A standing archer's balance issues can be caused by a variety of factors, including one leg being longer than the other, polio, one artificial leg, and so on. There are many options for dealing with this. The archer's shoe should be built up to compensate for a difference in leg length. If not, the coach will recommend placing a block of wood under the shorter leg to help build a strong, upright stance. If the archer's balance is bad, which may be dangerous if they fall over when it's windy, it's a good idea to recommend that they shoot from a chair or stool set up on the shooting line.


ARCHERS USING WHEELCHAIRS

CHAIR POSITION



Extra space on the shooting line to accommodate a wheelchair

The wheelchair should be positioned so that the archer's shoulders are facing the goal in the same way that a standing archer would.


The chair can be at 90 degrees to the target or angled towards the target, just like a standing archer's feet can be at 90 degrees to the target or angled towards the target if the coach believes it is helpful.


When on the shooting line, ensure that the archer applies the wheelchair brakes. If you're going outside and the ground is rough, make sure the archer has all four wheels in contact with the ground to keep the chair steady.


A wheelchair archer on the shooting line will need additional space.


ARCHER’S SITTING POSITION



A wheelchair archer using a body strap

Sitting balance varies considerably with wheelchair users. Those with very poor balance will benefit from a chest or lap strap to gain the extra support required for drawing the bow. Even those with better balance may wish to use a body strap initially until they have gained sufficient confidence for the coach to remove it.



Description:

The chair can be at 90 degrees to the target or angled towards the target, like the stance of standing archer.


ARCHER’S SITTING POSITION



Coaches must assist the archers to find some points of reference on the chair.

The chair back should be lower than the shoulder blades to avoid restricting the muscles used to draw the bow. The archers must place themselves in the chair exactly the same for each shot in order to achieve optimum results. The coach will help the archers locate some points of reference and teach them to double-check their location against those points on a regular basis.


As they draw the bow, archers will begin to lean back away from the target to compensate for a loss of balance, particularly as they fatigue. This form error can result in string clearance issues at the chest and in the wheelchair. Archer can fight this by leaning on the chair back for more stability.


Bow rest





If the archer has no upper-body impairment, the only equipment change that may be required is a shorter bow than usual due to the archer's proximity to the ground.


The archer may position a small foam or rubber pad in front of the wheel closest to the target to rest their bow arm and set the bottom limb tip down between shots.


Padded wheel





If the archer has no upper-body impairment, the only equipment change that may be required is a shorter bow than usual due to the archer's proximity to the ground.


The archer may position a small foam or rubber pad in front of the wheel closest to the target to rest their bow arm and set the bottom limb tip down between shots.
